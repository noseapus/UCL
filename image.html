1.1 Basic probability theory

1.1.1 Definitions

Definition (Sample space). Probability statements, made in the context of an experiment, may result in a number of possible outcomes, each of which may be denoted ω. The set of all possible outcomes is a sample space, denoted Ω.

Definition (Event). A collection of outcomes is an event. An event E occurs if the outcome of the experiment of E. E ⊆Ω.

1.1.2 Sets

Notation. For events E,F, recall that

  • Ec is the complement of E, the set of outcomes that are not in E.

  • E ∪ F is the union of E and F, the set of outcomes in E or in F (or both).

  • E ∩ F is the intersection of E and F, the set of outcomes in both E and F.

  • E ⊆ F denotes that E is a subset of F, so every element of E is in F.

  • E ⊂ F denotes that E is a subset of F, but E and F are not identical.

  • E∖F = E ∩ Fc is the set of elements of E that are not in F.

  • ∅ = {} = Ωc is the empty set, that which contains no elements.

  • If E ∩ F = ∅ then no outcomes are common to both E and F. These events are called disjoint, or mutually exclusive (m.e.)

  • If all possible pairs of events E1,… ⁡En are disjoint, they are called mutually disjoint or pairwise disjoint.

[ w]thmLaw[De Morgan’s] For events E,F,

(E ∩ F)c = Ec ∪ Fc (1.1) (E ∪ F)c = Ec ∩ Fc (1.2)

[ w]thmLaw[Distribution] For events E,F,G,

E ∩ (F ∪ G) = (E ∩ F) ∪ (E ∩ G) (1.3) E ∪ (F ∩ G) = (E ∪ F) ∩ (E ∪ G) (1.4)

1.1.4 Constructing probability functions

Remark. The axioms capture how probabilities behave intuitively, based on relative frequency (how many times an event occurs over many repetitions of an experiment), but allocation of useful probabilities rely on mathematical modelling. All results will be deduced from the axioms.

Method. For a countable sample space, specify probabilities for each individual ω ∈Ω. For Ω = {w1,w2,… ⁡}, let {p1,p2} be a set of ‘weights’ satisfying

pk ≥ 0,∑ k=1∞p k = 1

Then for any event E ⊆Ω,

P(E) = ∑ k:wk∈Epk

Clearly this satisfies equations (1.8) and (1.9). For equation (1.10), see that

P(E ∪ F) = ∑ i:ωi∈E∪Fpi = ∑ i:wi∈Epi + ∑ j:wj∈Fpj = P(E) + P(F)

Remark. For an uncountable sample space, construction of a probability function is more difficult, but the axioms still work.

1.1.6 Independence

Definition (Independent). Events E and F are independent if

P(E ∩ F) = P(E)P(F)

Events E1,… ⁡,En are mutually independent if, for any collection Ei1,… ⁡,Eik,

P(Ei1 ∩… ⁡Eik) = ∏ j=1kP(E ij)

Remark. Independence captures the idea that events are unrelated, that one does not affect the other.

Remark. For independent events E,F,

Mutually exclusive ⇔P(E ∩ F) = 0⇔P(E)P(F) = 0 ⇔P(E) = 0 or P(F) = 0

Remark. Mutual independence is a stronger condition than pairwise independence, which only requires pairs of events Ei,Ej to be independent.

1.1.7 Conditional probability

Definition (Conditional probability). The conditional probability of F given E is the probability that F occurs when E is known to have occurred. For P(E) > 0, it is given by

P(F|E) = P(E ∩ F) P(E)

Remark. The definition of conditional probability may be interpreted as changing the sample space from Ω to E, that is P(F|E) is the probability of F given that the outcome of the experiment is known to be in E. Note that P(E|E) = 1.

Remark. If E and F are independent, and P(E)≠0, then

P(F|E) = P(E ∩ F) P(E) = P(E)P(F) P(E) = P(F)

So E occurring does not change the chance of F occurring.

[ w]thmLaw[Total probability] Let {Ei} be a partition of Ω (the Eis are disjoint, and their union gives Ω). Then for any event F,

P(F) = ∑ iP(F|Ei)P(Ei)

So

P(F) = ∑ iP(F ∩ Ei) = ∑ iP(F|Ei)P(Ei) □
